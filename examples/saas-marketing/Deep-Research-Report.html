TLDR — 7 findings, leading with the most surprising

Distribution is the real moat — not the product8 creators AND 5 commenters independently said this. The product is 1% of the job; getting it in front of people is 99%. Bootstrappers have a 7-year survival rate of ~55% vs <5% for VC-backed competitors — the difference is distribution discipline, not capital.
Build in public is now table-stakes, not edge7 distinct creators say build-in-public flips your failure rate from 90% → 10%. RB2B went $0 → $1M ARR in 6 weeks on LinkedIn alone. The anomaly is that most founders STILL won't post daily — that's the actual gap, not 'finding the right channel.'
Cold email still works — but only with infrastructure most founders skip10 creators recommend it, but the working version requires: domain rotation (3-5 lookalike domains, 2 inboxes each), warm-up, sender pool, AND personalized hooks. Naked Gmail-to-Gmail cold email at 50/day is a known dead path — reply rates are now 3.4% (down from 8.5% in 2019).
Loom video DMs jump cold response rate from 5% → 20-30%Mentioned by only 2 creators and 1 commenter (Rok Bercic, Adam Robinson). This is anomaly territory — most founders never try video DMs. 4-6x lift over plain-text outreach. Hardest to scale, biggest leverage at low volume.
The 'stop doing X' framing beats 'AI-powered Y' messagingCommenter insight on Greg Isenberg's video: 'Tradespeople don't want AI software, they want to stop writing quotes at 9pm.' Frame value as what the customer can stop suffering, not the technology you built. Concrete pain > sexy capability.
Community-led growth beats both content and ads for B2B retention13 creators cite community as a top-3 channel. AllDayDevOps grew 0 → 13,000 members in 90 days with 2.5 part-time staff and zero vendor pitches. Slack groups and private Discords compound monthly; ads decay weekly.
AI search (ChatGPT/Perplexity referrals) is a real, underused channelAnomaly tactic — only 1 creator named it (Sam Dunning) but cited a 45K B2B lead from a single ChatGPT referral. Webflow data: LLM-referred users convert 6x higher than search-referred users. Most founders aren't even tracking this source yet.

Where creators disagree

Real disagreements in the corpus. These are where you have to make a judgment call — defaults are unsafe.

Reddit as a launch channel

Side A

Reddit drives high-quality leads via genuine engagement

Multiple creators report 20%+ of traffic and signups from organic Reddit comments and demos in /r/SaaS, /r/Entrepreneur

— Starter Story, Adam Robinson, Rok Bercic, Bishal Katuwal

Side B

Reddit will ban you fast if you self-promote — high risk channel

Mods are aggressive; even one perceived promotional comment gets you shadowbanned or permabanned across a subreddit

— Commenter (38 likes) on Wildfire Labs
Read: Both right. Reddit works if you spend weeks adding value before mentioning your product, and you accept losing accounts. Treat it as high-leverage but volatile.
Free first-customers strategy

Side A

Give the product free to your first 10 customers — speed > revenue

Free customers give honest feedback, drive testimonials, and create your case study library faster than paid ones

— Alex Hormozi, Alex Becker

Side B

Free is harder to give than cheap. Charge something — even $1 — to filter signal

Free users churn instantly when convenient. A paying customer at $5 reveals real willingness more than 100 free trials.

— Commenter (Alex Hormozi video), Rob Walling
Read: Free works for the first 5-10 as a deliberate testimonial-collection move. After that, even nominal pricing filters tire-kickers. Don't conflate free-for-feedback with freemium-as-strategy.
Traditional launches (Product Hunt / big-bang waitlist)

Side A

Product Hunt + waitlist drip = the standard zero-to-launch playbook

Multiple founders cite Product Hunt #1 days driving 1,000+ signups in 24 hours

— Rob Walling, Greg Isenberg, Starter Story

Side B

Big-bang launches are dead for AI/SaaS in 2026 — slow trickle wins

AI products are imperfect at launch. Public big-bang exposes flaws. Cohort drips let you stabilize between waves.

— TK Kader, Tom Ross (cohort model)
Read: Product Hunt still works as one touch — but the real engine is build-in-public + waitlist drip months in advance. Treat PH as one channel of many, not the launch.
Customer interviews vs pre-sales

Side A

Talk to customers — Mom Test interviews validate idea cheaply

Standard customer-development playbook from The Mom Test, classic startup advice

— Y Combinator-aligned, Rob Walling

Side B

Verbal validation is worthless. Only money in bank is proof.

Founders who relied on interviews built products no one paid for. Fake sales call + asking for credit card filters real demand.

— Commenter (Rob Walling video, 11 likes), Alex Becker
Read: Use interviews to find the pain. Use a pre-sale (or LOI / deposit) to validate willingness to pay. They're sequential, not alternatives.
Audience-first vs product-first

Side A

Build an audience BEFORE the product — distribution is the moat

RB2B hit $1M ARR in 6 weeks because LinkedIn audience pre-existed. Rand built Moz blog for 5 years before tool launch.

— Greg Isenberg, Adam Robinson, Rand Fishkin, Matt Gray

Side B

Audience-building takes years. Most founders don't have years. Ship first, market second.

ShipFast leaderboard shows products shipped fast can still find customers via SEO and Reddit without prior audience

— Marc Lou (implied), Pieter Levels (implied)
Read: If you already have any reach (even 200 followers in the right niche), use it. If you don't, ship + post-build-in-public — audience grows alongside revenue. Don't sequentialize them.

Methodology + Sources

How this was built

Phase 1: 1 Haiku 4.5 agent ran 15 YouTube searches across the topic, deduplicated, ranked by views × recency, and produced the 65-video master list.

Phase 2: 10 Sonnet 4.6 agents ran in parallel. Each pulled transcript + top 50 comments per video, extracted tactics tagged with source (creator vs commenter), stage, category, and confidence.

Phase 3: Opus 4.7 normalized 620 raw tactics into 69 canonical clusters via keyword matching with word-boundary safety, built creator/commenter frequency maps, computed co-occurrence edges, and classified into consensus/emerging/anomaly/commenter-only buckets.

Phase 4: This HTML. Phase 5: paired markdown notes in examples/saas-marketing/.

Skipped videos: 2 (transcripts too thin to extract from). Unclassified tactics: 204 (creator-specific advice that didn't match any canonical cluster — preserved in dataset.json for future review).
